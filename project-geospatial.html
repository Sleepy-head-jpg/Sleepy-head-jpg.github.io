Community Ecology · Master's Research

Himalayan Mixed-Species
Flock Dynamics

Status Active — Ongoing
Duration 2024 – present
Location Uttarakhand, India
Institution IISER Kolkata

Overview

Why do birds of different species flock together?

Mixed-species bird flocks are one of the most striking phenomena in tropical and subtropical forests. Dozens of birds — sometimes more than a dozen species — move through the canopy as a single coordinated unit. My research asks what holds these assemblages together, who leads them, and what happens when the forest they depend on starts to change.

Questions

What I'm trying to answer

The core questions driving this work are: Who are the nuclear species — the individuals other birds follow — in oak forests at different elevations? How does forest disturbance (logging, edge effects, plantation encroachment) reshape flock structure and species composition? And can flock integrity serve as a rapid indicator of habitat quality, something measurable in a morning's walk?

Methods

How I'm going about it

I conduct point-count and transect surveys at dawn across an elevational gradient in Uttarakhand, recording flock composition, size, and movement. Each flock encounter is logged with species list, nuclear species identity, habitat type, and disturbance index. Playback experiments are used to probe social cohesion between species pairs. Data analysis draws on network theory — treating each flock as a dynamic social graph.

Early Findings

What the data is starting to say

Early results suggest that nuclear species identity shifts with elevation and disturbance level — a pattern that, if robust, has implications for how we think about indicator species in montane systems. More field seasons needed before anything firmer can be said.
